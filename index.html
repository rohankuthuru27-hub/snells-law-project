📘 Introduction

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one medium to another due to a change in speed. This phenomenon is essential in understanding optical systems.

🌈 Electromagnetic Waves

Light is an electromagnetic wave that travels at a speed of 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum and exhibits properties like reflection and refraction.

🔍 Refractive Index

The refractive index is defined as the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum to that in a medium, expressed as n = c / v.

🔬 Snell’s Law

Snell’s Law explains the relationship between the angle of incidence and refraction using n₁ sinθ₁ = n₂ sinθ₂.

📐 Detailed Derivation of Snell’s Law

To derive Snell’s Law, we consider a light wave traveling from air into gelatin. When light enters a different medium, its speed changes, which causes bending.

Let the velocity of light in air be v₁ and in gelatin be v₂. Let the angles of incidence and refraction be θ₁ and θ₂.

According to wave theory, frequency remains constant while wavelength and velocity change.

v = fλ

Since frequency is constant:

v₁ / v₂ = λ₁ / λ₂

From geometry of wavefronts:

sinθ₁ / sinθ₂ = λ₁ / λ₂

Substituting:

sinθ₁ / sinθ₂ = v₁ / v₂

Using refractive index definition:

n = c / v

v₁ = c/n₁ , v₂ = c/n₂

Substitute:

sinθ₁ / sinθ₂ = (c/n₁) / (c/n₂)

Simplifying:

sinθ₁ / sinθ₂ = n₂ / n₁

Rearranging:

n₁ sinθ₁ = n₂ sinθ₂

Thus, Snell’s Law is derived, explaining refraction of light.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

n1 = 1.0
n2 = 1.35

theta1 = np.linspace(0, 80, 100)
theta2 = np.degrees(np.arcsin((n1/n2)*np.sin(np.radians(theta1))))

plt.plot(theta1, theta2)
plt.show()
